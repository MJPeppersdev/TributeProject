Gordon Moore

The co-founder of Intel, and creator of "Moore's Law"


A young Gordon Moore at Fairchild R & D in 1962.

    Here's a timeline of Gordon Moore's life:

  • 1929 - Born on January 3rd in Francisco, California

  • 1954 - Gordon earns his PhD from the 'California Institute of Technology' (Caltech).

  • 1957 - Moore was a part of a group of PhD graduates that parted ways with 'Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory'. They'd later be known as the 'Traitorous eight'.

  • 1958 - Was appointed Director of Research and Development (R&D) of 'Fairchild Semiconductor'.

  • 1965 - In an interview by the magazine 'Electronics' Moore made and observation regarding the future of semiconductor components, which has since been popularized as 'Moore's Law'.

  • 1968 - Founded 'NM Electronics' with business partner Robert Noyce. This company later went on to become the renowned 'Intel Corporation'.

  • 1975 - Named President of the Intel Corporation.

  • 1979 - Became Chief Executive Officer of Intel, and chaired the Board of Directors. He also served as a board member to 'Caltech'.

  • 1987 - Steps down as CEO of the Intel Corporation.

  • 1991 - Joins the biotechnology company 'Gilead Sciences' as a member of the Business Advisory Board. He was later made one of the members of the Board of Directors.

  • 1993 - Elected as Chairperson of the Board of Trustees of the 'California Institute of Technology' (Caltech). He is presently a life trustee of the institute.

  • 1997 - Was made the Chairman Emeritus of the Intel Corporation.

  • 2000 - Moore and his wife Betty co-found the 'Gordon and Betty Moore Foundation'. This organization focuses on conservation of the environment.

  • 2002 - Was given the 'Presidential Medal of Freedom' and later was the recipient of the 'Bower Award' for Business Leadership.

  • 2003 - Becomes a Fellow of the 'American Association for the Advancement of Science'.

  • 2008 - Is the recipient of the 'IEEE Medal of Honor' for his outstanding work in the field of telecommunications.

"With engineering, I view this year's failure as next year's opportunity to try it again. Failures are not something to be avoided. You want to have them happen as quickly as you can so you can make progress rapidly."

-- Gordon Moore
